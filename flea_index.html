Flea Market
My first few years in New Mexico was not easy for my family. My father was working long hours and we still couldn't live a comfortable life without having my mother also pitch in. I was so young at the time that I couldn't be in school, but day care was too expensive. So, my mother decided to work on her own accord until I could attend school. She sold baptism outfits and sets at the local flea market. We would get up at dawn to make sure we got a good spot at the flea market and we were there until sundown. She would make me Maruchan for lunch every day and we waited to eat dinner when we got home. It was a tiring lifestyle and I cannot image the life my mother would have had if she had not found a job working at the school I was later to attend.
I decided to talk about my roots through this project, the amount of Mexican people that work at flea markets is astounding. It is a life that is difficult and very risky. However, something I learned that hit deeper than the number of Mexicans that were there was how sweet and caring they all were. I approached a man and in Spanish asked if I could take his picture for a school project on Latinindad and in exchange I would give him five dollars. He nodded, and I went to take his picture. When I returned to give him the money, he refused and instead told me that being Latino means helping each other out.
